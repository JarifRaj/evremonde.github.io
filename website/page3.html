Entry One

I am utterly baffled. My capricious nephew has renounced his position as the Marquis!

As such, he will get his name removed from this site at my earliest convenience. Though, the situation would place myself in a predicament,

as now I have no heir. I don’t care a considerable amount since following my passing, I would not be present to care.

I’m rather poetic, aren’t I? I studied poetry in Brussels for a summer or two. Every Belgian knows me now.

Seriously, approach a Belgian, and ask them if they’re familiar with my work. Everyone will answer affirmatively, I tell you.

Regardless, my nephew seems to be strangely sympathetic with the dogs we profit off of. His sympathy befuddles me,

since, if the dogs weren’t so lazy, then they would have a deal of success in their lives. Unfortunately, they don’t,

so I am forced to make them work harder by raising my prices so they can hopefully acquire

a decent attitude towards work and finally accomplish something. I know, it is wishful thinking.

I, of course, acquired my immense fortune via nothing but dedication and hard work.

Along the way, I invented electricity, water, and candles. I am also an acclaimed inventor.

I am quite the genius, you see. My nephew doesn’t understand my vision with the peasantry,

he told me, what was it, “we did a world of wrong, injuring every human creature who came between us

and our pleasure, whatever it was.” (108). He thinks I’m doing this for my own pleasure! No, I am doing this for

the betterment of the dogs! My pleasure is worth more than theirs anyway.

Those who will not improve, they may remain obedient to the whip.

Those who do, they may live a better life, and maybe even become like me one day, although not as great as me.

I am doing a public service. I need to take my mind off of my former heir….

I also visited that gluttonous Monseigneur! O, what a beast he was! He looked like a giant toad sitting upright.

Although I must thank him for the spectacle. Those rooms were “adorned with every device of decoration

that the taste and skill of the time could achieve.” (92). It was astonishing to see. Someone so humble as myself couldn’t

have possibly stood there for more than a small amount of time, for I am not one to stoop as low as them in haughtiness.

I left, racing through the city streets. I was in fear for my life, then, you know.

It was dark and dangerous, indeed, I saw someone run over a child! Who could do such a thing?
Anyway, I went along, in my carriage, to my chateau and my nephew gave me that speech.
